## Priority A Verses (30) — First Month Essentials

These are the verses a new user encounters first. Each one should be immediately striking, practically applicable, and self-contained.

### 3. Gita 2.3
**Sanskrit:** *klaibyaṁ mā sma gamaḥ...*
**Why selected:** The bracing mentor voice that says: enough wallowing. This is the verse that turns compassion into a call to action -- the kind of direct honesty most people never receive.
**Intent:** courage, discipline | **Themes:** overcoming indecision, tough love, rising from self-pity
**Stoic parallel:** "How does it help, my husband, to make misfortune heavier by complaining about it? This is more fit for a king -- to seiz..." — Seneca, Oedipus, 80
**Bridge:** Both deliver the same unflinching message: self-pity is a form of cowardice, and the remedy is to stand up and engage.
**Best for:** When you have been grieving or deliberating long enough and need someone to say 'get up' -- the morning after a major setback.

### 5. Gita 2.11
**Sanskrit:** *aśocyān anvaśocas tvaṁ...*
**Why selected:** Krishna's opening gambit cuts through Arjuna's elaborate rationalizations. We all construct sophisticated arguments to avoid facing what we already know -- this verse exposes that pattern.
**Intent:** clarity, perspective | **Themes:** intellectual dishonesty, emotional avoidance disguised as reason, self-deception
**Stoic parallel:** "Throw out your conceited opinions, for it is impossible for a person to begin to learn what he thinks he already knows." — Epictetus, Discourses, 2.17.1
**Bridge:** Both expose the same trap: the person who speaks wisely while acting from confusion has substituted the appearance of knowledge for the real thing.
**Best for:** When you catch yourself arguing brilliantly for a position you know is wrong -- or when someone close to you is doing the same.

### 6. Gita 2.14
**Sanskrit:** *mātrā-sparśās tu kaunteya...*
**Why selected:** The foundational insight for emotional resilience: sensations, moods, and circumstances are weather systems that pass through you, not permanent features of your landscape. Endurance is a skill, not a trait.
**Intent:** patience, acceptance | **Themes:** impermanence of emotions, endurance, weather vs. climate
**Stoic parallel:** "To bear trials with a calm mind robs misfortune of its strength and burden." — Seneca, Hercules Oetaeus, 231-232
**Bridge:** Both teach that the power of difficulty is proportional to your resistance to it -- calm endurance diminishes suffering at its source.
**Best for:** During any period of discomfort that feels permanent -- a difficult boss, a health scare, a season of loneliness. The reminder: this too shall pass.

### 8. Gita 2.16
**Sanskrit:** *nāsato vidyate bhāvo...*
**Why selected:** The philosophical bedrock of the entire Gita: learn to distinguish what is truly real from what is merely appearing. Every clear decision depends on this capacity.
**Intent:** perspective, clarity | **Themes:** reality vs. illusion, discernment, foundational clear thinking
**Stoic parallel:** "Don't tell yourself anything more than what the initial impressions report. It's been reported to you that someone is sp..." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 8.49
**Bridge:** Both insist on separating what actually exists from the stories we overlay on top of it -- the real from the constructed.
**Best for:** When you need to cut through noise to find signal -- in decision-making, in conflict, in any moment where anxiety is manufacturing false realities.

### 10. Gita 2.22
**Sanskrit:** *vāsāṁsi jīrṇāni yathā...*
**Why selected:** The garment metaphor makes change feel natural rather than catastrophic. Career pivots, identity shifts, and life transitions are not endings -- they are wardrobe changes.
**Intent:** acceptance, perspective | **Themes:** change as renewal, life transitions, naturalness of transformation
**Stoic parallel:** "Meditate often on the swiftness with which all that exists and is coming into being is swept by us and carried away. For..." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 5.23
**Bridge:** Both strip the moral charge from change itself -- the Gita through the garment metaphor, Marcus through the river image -- both teaching that transformation is the nature of existence.
**Best for:** During any major life transition -- starting a new career, ending a relationship, moving cities, entering a new decade of life.

### 13. Gita 2.47
**Sanskrit:** *karmaṇy evādhikāras te...*
**Why selected:** The single most famous verse of the Gita and arguably the most universally applicable piece of wisdom in Indian philosophy. The antidote to both perfectionism and procrastination in one line.
**Intent:** discipline, clarity | **Themes:** detachment from outcomes, right to action alone, freedom from results-anxiety
**Stoic parallel:** "The chief task in life is simply this: to identify and separate matters so that I can say clearly to myself which are ex..." — Epictetus, Discourses, 2.5.4-5
**Bridge:** Both traditions draw the same fundamental line: your jurisdiction ends at the action itself -- everything beyond that belongs to forces outside your control.
**Best for:** Before any launch, submission, or performance where the outcome is uncertain -- the anxiety of 'will it work?' vs. 'am I doing the right thing?'

### 14. Gita 2.48
**Sanskrit:** *yoga-sthaḥ kuru karmāṇi...*
**Why selected:** The operational companion to 2.47: do the work, drop the scoreboard. This is the athlete's flow state, the entrepreneur's only sane operating mode, and the artist's path to their best work.
**Intent:** discipline, acceptance | **Themes:** flow state, detached engagement, equanimity in success and failure
**Stoic parallel:** "One person, on doing well by others, immediately accounts the expected favor in return. Another is not so quick, but sti..." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 5.6
**Bridge:** Both locate the measure of a life in the quality of one's engagement, not in the applause or results that follow -- act like the vine, and move on.
**Best for:** In the middle of a long project where results are far off -- when you need to find meaning in the daily work itself, not in the eventual outcome.

### 17. Gita 2.56
**Sanskrit:** *duḥkheṣv anudvigna-manāḥ...*
**Why selected:** The most precise character portrait of emotional mastery in the Gita -- undisturbed in sorrow, free from craving, beyond passion, fear, and anger. Specific, measurable, and aspirational without being preachy.
**Intent:** patience, discipline | **Themes:** emotional mastery, steadiness of mind, freedom from reactivity
**Stoic parallel:** "Who then is invincible? The one who cannot be upset by anything outside their reasoned choice." — Epictetus, Discourses, 1.18.21
**Bridge:** Both define the sage as someone whose inner state is not hostage to external events -- a person of radical sovereignty over their own reactions.
**Best for:** As a daily self-assessment tool: Am I undisturbed in sorrow today? Am I craving? Am I reactive? A mirror for the inner life.

### 18. Gita 2.62
**Sanskrit:** *dhyāyato viṣayān puṁsaḥ...*
**Why selected:** The 'downward spiral' mapped with clinical precision -- from rumination to attachment to desire to anger to confusion to the destruction of intelligence. Directly applicable to doom-scrolling, comparison traps, and addictive loops.
**Intent:** clarity, discipline | **Themes:** the downward spiral, rumination, how poor decisions cascade
**Stoic parallel:** "Every habit and capability is confirmed and grows in its corresponding actions, walking by walking, and running by runni..." — Epictetus, Discourses, 2.18.1-5
**Bridge:** Both map the mechanics of how mental patterns compound -- the Gita traces the downward spiral, Epictetus shows how habits self-reinforce in either direction.
**Best for:** When you notice yourself spiraling -- checking social media compulsively, ruminating on a slight, binge-consuming anything. The diagnostic that names the disease.

### 21. Gita 3.4
**Sanskrit:** *na karmaṇām anārambhān...*
**Why selected:** You cannot think your way to transformation -- you have to act. Withdrawal and endless analysis are not enlightenment; they are sophisticated forms of avoidance.
**Intent:** courage, discipline | **Themes:** action over withdrawal, freedom through engagement, anti-escapism
**Stoic parallel:** "Stop wandering about! You aren't likely to read your own notebooks, or ancient histories, or the anthologies you've coll..." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 3.14
**Bridge:** Both deliver the same rebuke to the eternal student: knowledge without action is a gilded cage, and the only rescue is doing.
**Best for:** When you have read enough books, attended enough workshops, and consumed enough content -- and still have not started the thing you know you need to do.

### 24. Gita 3.19
**Sanskrit:** *tasmād asaktaḥ satataṁ...*
**Why selected:** The daily practice distilled to its essence: show up, do the work, release the outcome. Every single day. This is not a philosophy -- it is an operating instruction.
**Intent:** discipline, patience | **Themes:** daily practice, consistent action, release of outcomes
**Stoic parallel:** "Never shirk the proper dispatch of your duty, no matter if you are freezing or hot, groggy or well-rested, vilified or p..." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 6.2
**Bridge:** Both traditions teach that virtue lies in the action itself, not in what follows -- and that the action must be performed regardless of conditions.
**Best for:** For anyone building a practice -- creative, athletic, spiritual, professional -- the verse to return to every morning when motivation is absent and only discipline remains.

### 28. Gita 3.35
**Sanskrit:** *śreyān sva-dharmo viguṇaḥ...*
**Why selected:** Stop comparing your path to someone else's. Your imperfect authentic work beats someone else's polished template. This verse is the antidote to impostor syndrome and comparison culture.
**Intent:** clarity, courage | **Themes:** authenticity over imitation, svadharma, your path vs. theirs
**Stoic parallel:** "Remember that you are an actor in a play, playing a character according to the will of the playwright -- if a short play..." — Epictetus, Enchiridion, 17
**Bridge:** Both insist that fulfillment comes from playing your role excellently, not from wishing you had been cast in someone else's.
**Best for:** When scrolling through others' highlight reels, when envying a colleague's trajectory, when tempted to abandon your own path for a more glamorous one.

### 33. Gita 4.42
**Sanskrit:** *tasmād ajñāna-sambhūtaṁ...*
**Why selected:** A battle cry for decisiveness: cut through doubt with the sword of knowledge and move. When you have deliberated enough, the remaining obstacle is not ignorance -- it is the habit of hesitation.
**Intent:** courage, clarity | **Themes:** decisiveness, cutting through doubt, arising to action
**Stoic parallel:** "But there is no reason to live and no limit to our miseries if we let our fears predominate." — Seneca, Moral Letters, 13.12b
**Bridge:** Both deliver the same ultimatum: at some point, continued deliberation becomes cowardice disguised as prudence -- and the only cure is to act.
**Best for:** The moment of decision after analysis paralysis -- when the research is done, the counsel is gathered, and the only thing left is to move.

### 38. Gita 5.23
**Sanskrit:** *śaknotīhaiva yaḥ soḍhuṁ...*
**Why selected:** Happiness is redefined as the capacity to withstand internal storms -- not the absence of them. This single reframe transforms the entire pursuit of wellbeing from accumulation to resilience.
**Intent:** discipline, patience | **Themes:** happiness as resilience, withstanding internal storms, redefining wellbeing
**Stoic parallel:** "I may wish to be free from torture, but if the time comes for me to endure it, I'll wish to bear it courageously with br..." — Seneca, Moral Letters, 67.4
**Bridge:** Both redefine the good life: not the absence of difficulty but the presence of the capacity to meet it without being destroyed.
**Best for:** When you realize that removing all problems will not make you happy -- and that building the inner strength to handle them will.

### 39. Gita 6.5
**Sanskrit:** *uddhared ātmanātmānaṁ...*
**Why selected:** You are simultaneously your own rescuer and your own saboteur. No guru, no system, no partner holds that power -- only you. The most empowering and terrifying truth in the text.
**Intent:** courage, discipline | **Themes:** self-rescue, self-sabotage, radical self-responsibility
**Stoic parallel:** "The raw material for the work of a good and excellent person is their own guiding reason, the body is that of the doctor..." — Epictetus, Discourses, 3.3.1
**Bridge:** Both traditions identify the self as both the raw material and the craftsman -- you are the project and the worker simultaneously.
**Best for:** When you realize no one is coming to save you -- and that this realization is not despair but the beginning of genuine power.

### 40. Gita 6.6
**Sanskrit:** *bandhur ātmātmanas tasya...*
**Why selected:** Self-mastery converts your inner life from battleground to ally. The relationship you have with yourself is the one relationship that determines all others.
**Intent:** clarity, discipline | **Themes:** the self as friend or enemy, self-mastery, inner alliance
**Stoic parallel:** "I'm constantly amazed by how easily we love ourselves above all others, yet we put more stock in the opinions of others ..." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 12.4
**Bridge:** Both point to the paradox of self-relationship: we are simultaneously our most intimate companion and our most neglected one.
**Best for:** When self-talk is destructive, when inner criticism is louder than any external voice -- the verse that names the internal relationship as the one that matters most.

### 45. Gita 6.26
**Sanskrit:** *yato yato niścalati...*
**Why selected:** The foundational instruction of every meditation tradition: the mind wanders, you bring it back. That IS the practice. Not the failure of practice -- the practice itself.
**Intent:** discipline, patience | **Themes:** meditation instruction, the wandering mind, returning as practice
**Stoic parallel:** "This is the true athlete -- the person in rigorous training against false impressions. Remain firm, you who suffer, don'..." — Epictetus, Discourses, 2.18.27-28
**Bridge:** Both frame mental discipline as an athletic endeavor -- the struggle is not a sign of failure but the very exercise through which mastery is built.
**Best for:** For anyone who has tried meditation and concluded they are 'bad at it' because their mind wanders -- the verse that says: the wandering IS normal, the returning IS the practice.

### 60. Gita 11.32
**Sanskrit:** *kālo 'smi loka-kṣaya-kṛt...*
**Why selected:** The ultimate perspective check: 'I am time, the great destroyer.' This is not nihilism -- it is liberation from the illusion of permanence. Oppenheimer quoted it for good reason; it remains the Gita's most viscerally powerful moment.
**Intent:** perspective, acceptance | **Themes:** time as destroyer, radical impermanence, liberation through perspective
**Stoic parallel:** "Both Alexander the Great and his mule-keeper were both brought to the same place by death -- they were either received i..." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 6.24
**Bridge:** Both use the finality of time and death to dissolve the illusion that status, achievement, or permanence can be secured -- everything returns to the same place.
**Best for:** When you are taking yourself, your problems, or your achievements too seriously -- the cosmic cold shower that resets perspective.

### 61. Gita 11.33
**Sanskrit:** *tasmāt tvam uttiṣṭha...*
**Why selected:** Stop agonizing over outcomes that are already unfolding. Your job is to show up and act -- you are the instrument, not the author. The single most galvanizing verse in the Gita.
**Intent:** courage, acceptance | **Themes:** arise and act, instrument not author, surrender to the larger process
**Stoic parallel:** "Difficulties show a person's character. So when a challenge confronts you, remember that God is matching you with a youn..." — Epictetus, Discourses, 1.24.1-2
**Bridge:** Both reframe the call to action: you are not being punished by what is asked of you -- you are being shaped by it, and your role is to rise to the occasion.
**Best for:** The verse to read before the battle, the launch, the confrontation, the surgery, the speech -- any moment where you must act despite uncertainty about the outcome.

### 64. Gita 12.12
**Sanskrit:** *śreyo hi jñānam abhyāsāj...*
**Why selected:** A hierarchy of practices ending with the ultimate: let go of results. If you can do only one thing, do that. The verse that ranks all spiritual practices and puts non-attachment at the top.
**Intent:** acceptance, clarity | **Themes:** hierarchy of practices, renunciation of fruits, peace through letting go
**Stoic parallel:** "Don't seek for everything to happen as you wish it would, but rather wish that everything happens as it actually will --..." — Epictetus, Enchiridion, 8
**Bridge:** Both arrive at the same destination by different routes: the highest practice is alignment with what is, rather than insistence on what should be.
**Best for:** When overwhelmed by too many spiritual or self-improvement practices -- the verse that simplifies everything to a single instruction: release your grip on results.

### 65. Gita 12.13
**Sanskrit:** *adveṣṭā sarva-bhūtānāṁ...*
**Why selected:** The most detailed character portrait of the ideal person in the Gita -- friendly, compassionate, free from ego, equal in pain and pleasure, forgiving, content, self-controlled. A practical checklist for self-assessment.
**Intent:** patience, discipline | **Themes:** the ideal character, self-assessment checklist, comprehensive virtue
**Stoic parallel:** "What is your vocation? To be a good person." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 11.5
**Bridge:** The Gita provides the detailed blueprint; Marcus Aurelius provides the summary -- both converge on the same destination: becoming a genuinely good human being is the entire project.
**Best for:** As an annual or quarterly self-review: read the list of qualities, honestly assess where you stand on each, and identify the one or two to focus on next.

### 68. Gita 12.18
**Sanskrit:** *samaḥ śatrau ca mitre ca...*
**Why selected:** Equal toward enemy and friend, in honor and dishonor, in cold and heat, happiness and suffering. Stoic equanimity rendered in complete detail -- a verse Marcus Aurelius could have written.
**Intent:** acceptance, patience | **Themes:** equanimity in all conditions, equal toward opposites, universal steadiness
**Stoic parallel:** "The task of a philosopher: we should bring our will into harmony with whatever happens, so that nothing happens against ..." — Epictetus, Discourses, 2.14.7
**Bridge:** Both describe the same aspiration: a person so aligned with reality that no circumstance -- good or bad, pleasant or painful -- can dislodge their composure.
**Best for:** When life hands you the full spectrum in rapid succession -- praise followed by criticism, success followed by failure -- and you need a single standard for how to meet it all.

### 71. Gita 13.34
**Sanskrit:** *ksetra-ksetrajnayor evam...*
**Why selected:** The fundamental insight of the Gita's metaphysics: you are not your thoughts, body, or circumstances -- you are the awareness observing them. This single distinction, when grasped experientially, changes everything.
**Intent:** clarity, perspective | **Themes:** awareness vs. content, observer and observed, liberation through discernment
**Stoic parallel:** "Today I escaped from the crush of circumstances, or better put, I threw them out, for the crush wasn't from outside me b..." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 9.13
**Bridge:** Both Krishna and Marcus locate liberation in the same move: recognizing that you are not the field of experience but the awareness that observes it, and that the 'crush' lives in your assumptions, not in the world.
**Best for:** When you are over-identified with a role, a body, or a set of thoughts. When you need to step back and remember what you actually are.

### 78. Gita 16.1
**Sanskrit:** *abhayaṁ sattva-saṁśuddhir...*
**Why selected:** The complete inventory of divine qualities -- fearlessness first, then purity, steadfastness, charity, self-control, honesty, non-violence, truth, compassion. This is the Gita's most comprehensive character development checklist, and fearlessness leads for a reason.
**Intent:** courage, discipline | **Themes:** divine qualities, character inventory, fearlessness
**Stoic parallel:** "Do you then ponder how the supreme of human evils, the surest mark of the base and cowardly, is not death, but the fear ..." — Epictetus, Discourses, 3.26.38-39
**Bridge:** Both the Gita and Epictetus place fearlessness at the foundation of all virtue -- without courage, no other quality can fully manifest.
**Best for:** When assessing your character honestly. When you want a concrete list of qualities to develop, ordered by priority.

### 79. Gita 16.21
**Sanskrit:** *tri-vidham narakasyedam...*
**Why selected:** Three gates of self-destruction: desire, anger, and greed. A ruthlessly clear diagnostic. If any of these three is driving your decisions, you are heading toward ruin. The simplicity is the power.
**Intent:** clarity, discipline | **Themes:** three gates of hell, self-destruction, diagnostic clarity
**Stoic parallel:** "Keep this thought handy when you feel a fit of rage coming on--it isn't manly to be enraged. Rather, gentleness and civi..." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 11.18.5b
**Bridge:** Both Krishna and Marcus identify anger as a gate to self-destruction and redefine strength: true power is not the capacity for rage but the capacity for calm when rage is calling.
**Best for:** When you suspect that desire, anger, or greed is behind a decision you are about to make. When you need a simple test to run before acting.

### 92. Gita 18.37
**Sanskrit:** *yat tad agre viṣam iva...*
**Why selected:** The most practical wisdom in the Gita's final chapter: what feels like poison at first becomes nectar. Every worthwhile practice is hard at the beginning and rewarding later. Every cheap pleasure is the reverse. Choose accordingly.
**Intent:** patience, discipline | **Themes:** delayed gratification, poison to nectar, long-term thinking
**Stoic parallel:** "Success comes to the lowly and to the poorly talented, but the special characteristic of a great person is to triumph ov..." — Seneca, On Providence, 4.1
**Bridge:** Both Krishna and Seneca affirm that difficulty at the beginning is not a sign of a wrong path -- true greatness is forged precisely by enduring what feels like poison until the nectar appears.
**Best for:** When a new discipline feels terrible and you want to quit. When you need to trust the process through the bitter beginning.

### 94. Gita 18.47
**Sanskrit:** *sreyan sva-dharmo vigunah...*
**Why selected:** Repeated from 3.35 in the final chapter for emphasis: your own imperfect path is superior to another's perfect one. This is the Gita's most urgent plea against comparison, imitation, and inauthenticity.
**Intent:** clarity, courage | **Themes:** svadharma, authenticity, stop comparing
**Stoic parallel:** "What's left to be prized? This, I think--to limit our action or inaction to only what's in keeping with the needs of our..." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 6.16.2b-4a
**Bridge:** Both Krishna and Marcus insist that you must prize your own preparation, your own mind, your own path -- not someone else's. Harmony comes from honoring your own dharma, not from imitating another's.
**Best for:** When envying someone else's career, talent, or life. When comparison paralysis prevents you from committing to your own work.

### 95. Gita 18.48
**Sanskrit:** *saha-jaṁ karma kaunteya...*
**Why selected:** All action is tainted by some fault, as fire is tainted by smoke. Imperfection is inherent to every endeavor. This is not a reason to quit -- it is a reason to begin. The verse demolishes perfectionism as an excuse for inaction.
**Intent:** courage, acceptance | **Themes:** imperfection is inherent, anti-perfectionism, begin anyway
**Stoic parallel:** "Don't behave as if you are destined to live forever. What's fated hangs over you. As long as you live and while you can,..." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 4.17
**Bridge:** Both Krishna and Marcus cut through the paralysis of perfectionism with the same urgency: you will never do it perfectly, and you are running out of time. Start now.
**Best for:** When perfectionism prevents you from shipping, publishing, starting, or committing. When you need permission to begin despite knowing the result will be imperfect.

### 97. Gita 18.63
**Sanskrit:** *iti te jñānam ākhyātaṁ...*
**Why selected:** The ultimate respect for human autonomy: even after delivering the entire Gita, Krishna says 'I have told you what I know -- now think it through and decide for yourself.' No coercion, no ultimatum. The highest teaching ends in freedom.
**Intent:** clarity, courage | **Themes:** autonomy, personal responsibility, freedom of choice
**Stoic parallel:** "Show me that the good life doesn't consist in its length, but in its use, and that it is possible--no, entirely too comm..." — Seneca, Moral Letters, 49.10b
**Bridge:** Both Krishna and Seneca treat the listener as a sovereign agent who must ultimately choose how to use what they have been given -- wisdom offered freely, decision left to you.
**Best for:** When you have received enough counsel and need to decide. When the time for learning ends and the time for choosing begins.

### 98. Gita 18.66
**Sanskrit:** *sarva-dharmān parityajya...*
**Why selected:** The final instruction of the entire Gita: after all analysis, practice, and discernment, the ultimate move is surrender. Not passive resignation but active trust in something greater than the calculating mind.
**Intent:** acceptance, courage | **Themes:** surrender, ultimate trust, beyond analysis
**Stoic parallel:** "There is no evil in things changing, just as there is no good in persisting in a new state." — Marcus Aurelius, Meditations, 4.42
**Bridge:** Both Krishna and Marcus arrive at the same place of radical surrender: release all fixed positions, abandon all insistence that things be a certain way, and trust the unfolding of reality itself.
**Best for:** When you have done everything you can and must release. When all effort has been exhausted and only trust remains.

## Selection Notes

### Chapter balance
- **Chapters 1-6** yielded the most selections (48) because they contain the Gita's most concentrated practical philosophy: karma yoga, the sthitaprajna ideal, meditation instruction, and the foundational action-without-attachment teaching.
- **Chapters 7-12** (20 selections) are the most devotional section. We selected only verses with clear secular/universal applicability, such as 11.32-33 ("I am time" / "Arise and be the instrument") and the character portrait sequence in Chapter 12.
- **Chapters 13-18** (32 selections) provide the synthesis: the guna framework (Ch 14), the three gates of hell (16.21), sattvic happiness (18.37), and the magnificent closing sequence where Krishna grants Arjuna full autonomy (18.63) before the final surrender (18.66).

### Stoic pairing approach
- All 100 Stoic quotes are from original sources: Marcus Aurelius (*Meditations*), Seneca (*Moral Letters*, *On the Shortness of Life*, *On Anger*, etc.), and Epictetus (*Discourses*, *Enchiridion*).
- Marcus Aurelius is somewhat overrepresented (47 quotes) due to the *Meditations* being the richest single source of quotable Stoic wisdom with direct Gita resonance. Seneca (27) and Epictetus (26) provide strong secondary coverage.
- Every pairing was selected for genuine philosophical overlap, not surface similarity. The theme_bridge field articulates the specific point of convergence.

### Excluded categories
- Specific worship instructions and deity descriptions (much of Ch 7-10)
- Detailed cosmological frameworks (Ch 8 on different planes of existence)
- Caste-specific instructions that require historical context
- Purely metaphysical verses with no practical application

### Notable inclusions
- **1.28 and 1.47** (Arjuna's crisis): Though not "wisdom verses," they frame the entire text as a response to real human paralysis — essential for the app's narrative arc.
- **2.62-63** (the downward spiral): Grouped as one entry because the two verses form a single, inseparable chain of causation.
- **18.63** ("act as you see fit"): One of the most remarkable verses in any religious text — the divine teacher telling the student to think for himself.
- **18.66** ("abandon all dharmas"): The final paradox — after 700 verses of detailed instruction, the last word is surrender.
